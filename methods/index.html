Search strategy and selection criteria

References were identified through structured searches of PubMed, ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, and Scopus, with records captured up to April 30, 2026. No lower limit on publication date was specified a priori. After screening, the 427 included studies ranged from January 2010 to April 2026. Free-text search terms combined AI concepts ("artificial intelligence", "machine learning", and "deep learning") with task terms ("classification", "detection", "segmentation", "monitoring", "prediction", and "screening") and ophthalmic imaging or disease terms ("optical coherence tomography", "OCT", "OCTA", "ophthalm*", "retina*", "macula*", "glaucoma", "diabetic retinopathy", and "age-related macular degeneration"). Eligible records addressed AI-based methods in an ophthalmic context, involved OCT, OCTA, or related ophthalmic imaging data, and examined a clinically or translationally relevant task; this imaging requirement defined the review scope. Non-English publications, duplicates, and records outside the scope of AI in ophthalmology were excluded. The 427-article evidence map is distinct from the broader reference list, which also includes contextual sources.

Three reviewers (TS, MF, and RV) screened titles and abstracts using predefined exclusion criteria. At least two authors were involved in eligibility decisions, and disagreements or borderline cases were resolved by consensus. Articles that passed title and abstract screening underwent full-text assessment. The final reference list was constructed on the basis of eligibility, relevance, methodological importance, and contribution to the thematic, ethical, translational, or evidence mapping synthesis. The review was reported according to PRISMA principles where applicable to this heterogeneous translational review. Article-level mappings, public dataset overview, and extended synthesis are provided in the supplementary material.

Evidence synthesis and mapping approach

The literature was synthesized descriptively and qualitatively, complemented by keyword co-occurrence analysis, international co-authorship mapping, public dataset mapping, and evidence gap mapping. For each publication, we extracted article type, ophthalmic condition or clinical context, AI methodology, intended AI role, data-related challenges, implementation barriers, and ethics, governance, or regulatory issues. Extraction and category assignments were reviewed by at least two authors; disagreements were resolved through consensus discussion rather than numerical voting, because the task required categorical interpretation. The supplementary article-level tables provide the audit trail from extracted fields, contribution summaries, and category assignments to aggregate counts. Evidence gap maps were constructed at article-assignment level and treated as a central interpretive component. General dimensions were categorized as predominantly positive, predominantly negative, or balanced; positive meant enabling the dimension, negative meant creating a barrier or risk, and balanced meant both. For ethics, governance, or regulatory dimensions, "caused" meant AI introduced or amplified an issue, "resolved" meant AI mitigated one, and "both" captured mixed cases such as access gains with bias concerns. Multiple assignments per publication were permitted. These mappings anchored the interpretive synthesis, were not intended to provide pooled diagnostic accuracy estimates, risk-of-bias judgments, or certainty-of-evidence ratings, and provide an explicit bridge between article-level evidence and the roadmap for future research.

Strengths and limitations

This review combines a structured search with thematic analysis, keyword co-occurrence mapping, international co-authorship analysis, public dataset mapping, and evidence gap mapping. This approach provides a broader perspective than a narrative review focused only on selected applications or model performance. The supplementary material provides article-level mappings and dataset documentation to support transparency and traceability.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, no lower limit on publication date was specified a priori, but the article-level evidence map ultimately included studies from January 2010 to April 2026. This window reflects eligible literature captured by the search and screening process, not a deliberately bounded review beginning in 2010; studies after the April 30, 2026 search cutoff may alter the balance of evidence. Second, the review was imaging-dependent because eligibility required OCT, OCTA, or related ophthalmic imaging data, and therefore does not fully capture non-imaging ophthalmic AI such as administrative NLP or operational analytics. Third, only English-language publications were included, which may have excluded relevant work in a globally distributed field. Fourth, heterogeneity in article type, clinical indication, AI task, imaging modality, dataset source, validation strategy, and outcome reporting limited comparability and precluded meta-analysis. Fifth, evidence gap maps summarize article-level orientations rather than standardized effect sizes, diagnostic accuracy estimates, risk-of-bias assessments, or certainty ratings. Finally, many studies provided limited information on sex, gender, race, ethnicity, socioeconomic position, or other dimensions of representativeness.

Source selection and copyright

Manuscript files are matched by exact citation key before any identifier or title fallback. When alternatives exist, the deterministic quality order is PDF full text, structured HTML or XML full text, PDF-derived text, TXT full text, and abstract-only text. Structured HTML or XML is preferred over PDF-derived plain text when it preserves cleaner section structure. Landing pages are excluded.

Local availability is not treated as redistribution permission. Public full text is rendered only when the selected source is structured full text and the repository records an explicit CC BY or CC0 licence. Unknown licence status defaults to abstract-only or external-link-only publication. Publisher PDFs and restricted source files are never copied into the public build.

Website generation

A Python preprocessing pipeline resolves LaTeX inputs, parses BibTeX without regenerating keys, matches article files, normalizes explicit metadata arrays, aggregates evidence assignments, and emits validation reports. Astro then generates static article and dataset routes. Vega-Lite renders interactive charts from the normalized layer, and Pagefind indexes the completed static HTML.

The production build contains no database, authentication, analytics, cookies, third-party scripts, or runtime API calls. Expensive visualizations load only on their own pages, and restricted full text is excluded before the frontend build starts.

Known limitations

  • One included study is absent from the older merged extraction table and download manifest, so unsupported enrichment fields remain empty.
  • Machine-readable keyword-network nodes and edges and country-level collaboration counts are not present in the repository. The authored figures are shown without reverse-engineered numerical data.
  • Condition, modality, method, task, validation, and dataset facets use conservative explicit-text rules applied to curated article summaries and abstracts. They are navigational aids, not new scientific adjudications.
  • Country filters use explicit affiliation text only and never infer geography from author names.
  • Absence of a coded value means that the repository did not support a reliable assignment; it does not prove absence in the underlying study.
